# Safe Use Cases

Tracewright is useful when a reviewer wants to slow down judgment, separate source roles, and prepare responsible next checks.

## Review An Article Before Citing It

Use Tracewright to identify central claims, source-backed details, unsupported assertions, and follow-up checks before relying on an article.

## Check Whether An Academic Draft Overclaims

Use Academic Review mode to inspect research claims, method description, citation support, limitations, and generalization beyond evidence.

## Review A Public Narrative Dossier

Use Tracewright to compare public statements, company pages, reporting, transcripts, and later interpretation without collapsing them into one voice.

## Analyze Source Roles In Provenance Materials

Use Provenance / Source-Role Review to separate ownership history, attribution evidence, archive gaps, material analysis, researcher opinion, and catalogue records.

## Check Correspondence Continuity Without Accusing The Writer

Use Correspondence / Personal Narrative Continuity mode to inspect response alignment, remembered details, style shifts, and disclosed workflow while avoiding person-level accusations.

## Compare Institutional Statements Over Time

Use Tracewright to map what changed, what stayed stable, which statements are role-bound, and what evidence would be needed before claiming contradiction.

## Prepare A Human Review Checklist

Use Evidence Cards and Follow-Up Questions to prepare a checklist for a teacher, editor, researcher, provenance specialist, lawyer, HR reviewer, or other qualified human decision-maker.

## Improve Human-Written Work

Use Tracewright to clarify arguments, identify weak links, separate evidence from intuition, and decide what should be revised before publication, submission, citation, or escalation.
